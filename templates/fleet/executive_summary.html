Anomaly Tiers
Why this matters: Early identification of battery degradation enables proactive interventions that prevent on-route breakdowns, trigger timely warranty claims before they expire, and support AMC (Annual Maintenance Contract) requests with hard evidence. Catching issues early also protects operational uptime — a battery failure mid-route is far costlier than a scheduled maintenance stop.
Outlier Detection (IF)
Spots sudden anomalies — flags sessions where the combination of charge level, temperature, cell balance, and internal resistance looks unusual compared to normal behaviour. One bad session in isolation. Think: "this session was abnormal."
Drift Tracking (CUSUM)
Identifies gradual wear — detects slow, sustained deterioration building up over many sessions, such as steadily rising internal resistance or creeping cell imbalance that no single session makes obvious. Think: "this vehicle is on a declining trend."

Frequently Asked Questions

State of Health (SoH) measures how much of the battery's original capacity remains. 100% = brand new; 80% = end of usable life for commercial EV operation.

Coulomb counting is the foundational method: integrate the current in/out of the battery over time to measure how many Amp-hours (Ah) were actually stored or delivered, then compare against the original rated capacity (436 Ah for this fleet). A session that delivers 420 Ah when the battery started full → SoH ≈ 420/436 ≈ 96.3%.

Cycle SoH (shown as "Cycle SOH" column): The SoH estimated from one complete discharge or charge cycle. For discharge: (Ah discharged) / (nominal capacity × SoC change). Requires a session where the battery goes through enough SoC range to measure reliably. Quality-gated — sessions with very small SoC swings are excluded.

Block SoH: Uses the full drive-to-charge block (multiple sessions) rather than a single trip. The "Block Cap AH" column shows the total Ah across the block; dividing by the SoC drop gives an effective pack capacity, which is then divided by the nominal 436 Ah to get SoH. This is more robust than single-session estimates because it averages out trip-to-trip variation.

EKF SoH: The most reliable estimate — uses an Extended Kalman Filter that combines Cycle SoH, BMS-reported SoH, internal resistance, cell spread, and temperature signals to maintain a continuously updated, noise-filtered health estimate for each vehicle.

Weak subsystem refers to the battery subsystem (group of cells) with the lowest average voltage — the "weakest link" that limits overall pack performance.

Consistency score (0–1) measures how often the same subsystem is the weakest across sessions. A score near 1.0 means the same group is consistently the weakest — a localised, identifiable problem worth targeted inspection. A score near 0 means the weakest subsystem changes frequently — either measurement noise, very even cell ageing (no clear weak spot), or transient thermal effects.

How to interpret: Low consistency + high cell spread = measurement-level noise or distributed ageing. Low consistency + low spread = generally balanced pack. High consistency + low spread = one persistently weak group that should be inspected at the next service. High consistency + high spread = clear candidate for cell replacement.

Why subsystem voltage std is high: Each subsystem averages the voltages of its constituent cells. If one group has cells at the end of their plateau and another is still in the middle, the difference can be 5–20 mV even in a "healthy" pack. Under load (discharge), these differences amplify because weaker cells sag more. So a high std during discharge is normal; a persistently high std at rest or during charging is the flag to watch.

Thermal stress is a composite index that captures both the intensity and duration of high-temperature exposure during a session. It is computed as:

thermal_stress = (temp_max − temp_threshold) × duration_above_threshold_hr

where temp_threshold is the fleet-calibrated safe operating temperature (typically 40–45°C). Sessions where the pack stays cool have near-zero thermal stress. Sessions with sustained high temperatures (e.g. heavy-load runs in summer) accumulate high stress scores.

Why it matters: Lithium-ion chemistry degrades faster at elevated temperatures — both calendar ageing and cycle ageing accelerate above 35°C. Sustained thermal stress is one of the strongest predictors of accelerated SoH decline in this fleet's Bayesian Ridge model.

Isolation Forest (IF) — session-level outlier detection. It looks at the combination of 30+ signals in a single session and asks: "Is this session statistically unusual compared to all sessions in the fleet?" Flags isolated bad events.

CUSUM (Cumulative Sum) — drift detection. It tracks individual signals (IR, cell spread, temperature, SoH, voltage sags, efficiency) across consecutive sessions and raises an alarm when there is a sustained upward or downward shift — even if no single session is an outlier. Catches slow, persistent degradation trends.

The combination means the system catches both sudden shocks (charger faults, unusual trips) and gradual decline that would be invisible in a snapshot view.
